Numbers that Describe Data

Position (Mean, Median, Mode) and Variation (Range, Standard Deviation)
Target Students: High School (First Year) / Early English Learners
What you need to know: Simple math (+, -, ×, ÷) and how to read a histogram.
The Big Picture (L'obiettivo): Imagine you have a list of data, like the test scores of your class. Looking at a long list of numbers is confusing. A histogram helps because it shows the "shape" of the data. But numbers help us even more! In statistics, we use two types of numbers:
  1. Numbers that show the center of the data (Position).
  2. Numbers that show how spread out the data is (Variation).

Part 1: Finding the Center (Indices of Position)

These indices help us find the middle or the most common value of our data. The three main tools are the Mean (Media), the Median (Mediana), and the Mode (Moda).

1. The Mean (Media)

This is the normal average. To find it, add all the numbers together. Then, divide the total by how many numbers there are.

Mean = (Sum of all numbers) ÷ (How many numbers)

Idea: Imagine your data points are weights on a see-saw (altalena). The mean is the exact point in the middle where the see-saw stays perfectly flat and balanced.

2. The Median (Mediana)

The median is the exact middle number. First, you must sort your numbers from smallest to largest. Then look at the middle:

  • If you have an odd number of items (e.g., 5 or 7 numbers): The median is the single number directly in the middle.
  • If you have an even number of items (e.g., 6 or 8 numbers): There are two middle numbers. The median is the average of those two numbers.

3. The Mode (Moda)

The mode is the number that appears most frequently (the most popular number). On a histogram, the mode is always the tallest bar!

Example: Quiz Scores

Seven students take a short test. Their scores are: 8, 5, 9, 5, 10, 7, 5.

Step 1: Sort the numbers from smallest to largest!

Sorted Data: 5, 5, 5, 7, 8, 9, 10

  • Mean (Media): Add them: 5 + 5 + 5 + 7 + 8 + 9 + 10 = 49.
    Divide by 7 students: 49 ÷ 7 = 7. So, the Mean = 7.
  • Median (Mediana): Look at the sorted list: 5, 5, 5, 7, 8, 9, 10.
    The middle number is 7. So, the Median = 7.
  • Mode (Moda): The number 5 appears three times. The others appear only once. So, the Mode = 5.
Figure 1: In a balanced, symmetric histogram, the Mean and Median sit perfectly together in the center.
Figure 2: When there is an extreme outlier (like the score 10 here), the Mean gets pulled up, while the Median stays more stable.
💡 Quick Note: The Outlier Danger
The Mean can change a lot because of one extreme number (called an outlier). For example, if one student gets a 0, the Mean drops heavily, but the Median stays almost the same. This is why when we talk about salaries or house prices, we usually use the Median.

Part 2: Measuring the Spread (Indices of Variation)

Two groups can have the same mean but look completely different. For example, Class A has scores like 7, 7, 7, 7 (Mean = 7). Class B has scores like 4, 5, 9, 10 (Mean = 7). Class B is much more spread out! We use variation to measure this.

1. The Range (Intervallo)

The simplest way to measure spread. Take the biggest number and subtract the smallest number.

Range = Maximum Number − Minimum Number

For our quiz scores: 10 − 5 = 5. The Range is 5.

2. The Standard Deviation (deviazione standard)

This shows the average distance between each data point and the mean.

  • Low standard deviation: The numbers are very close to the mean. The histogram looks narrow and tall.
  • High standard deviation: The numbers are far away from the mean. The histogram looks wide and flat.
Figure 3: Low variation. The values are tightly packed around the center. Range is small and standard deviation is low.
Figure 4: High variation. The values are spread wide away from the center, making the profile low and flat.

How to Calculate Standard Deviation Step-by-Step

Let's use our quiz scores from before: 5, 5, 5, 7, 8, 9, 10. We already know the Mean = 7.

Score (x) Distance from Mean (Score − 7) Squared Distance (Distance2)
5 5 − 7 = −2 (−2)2 = 4
5 5 − 7 = −2 (−2)2 = 4
5 5 − 7 = −2 (−2)2 = 4
7 7 − 7 = 0 02 = 0
8 8 − 7 = 1 12 = 1
9 9 − 7 = 2 22 = 4
10 10 − 7 = 3 32 = 9
Total Numbers: 7 Sum is 0 Sum of Squares = 26

Why do we square the distances? Look at the middle column. If we add those numbers, they become 0 because positive and negative numbers cancel out! Squaring makes every number positive.

  1. Step 1: Find the mean (We know it is 7).
  2. Step 2: Find the distance from the mean for each score (Middle column).
  3. Step 3: Square each distance to make it positive (Right column).
  4. Step 4: Find the average of those squared numbers.
    26 ÷ 7 = 3.71 (This is called the Variance)
  5. Step 5: Take the square root (√) to undo the squaring.
    Standard Deviation = √3.71 ≈ 1.93

What it means: On average, the student scores are 1.93 points away from the mean score of 7.

Summary Cheat Sheet

  • Mean (Media): The balance point. Affected by extreme numbers.
  • Median (Mediana): The exact middle number. Safe from extreme numbers.
  • Mode (Moda): The most common number. The highest bar on a histogram.
  • Range (Intervallo): Max minus Min. A quick look at the total spread.
  • Standard Deviation (Deviazione Standard): The best measure for how spread out or close the data points are.

The daily high temperature (in °C) during one week in spring was recorded as:

15, 17, 14, 15, 19, 21, 14

Your Tasks:

  1. Sort the temperatures from coldest to warmest.
  2. Find the Mean, Median, and Mode temperature.
  3. Calculate the Range (Intervallo).

A local pizzeria tracks how many minutes it takes to deliver 5 orders: 20, 25, 22, 30, 28 minutes.

Your Tasks:

  1. Find the Mean time.
  2. Calculate the standard deviation using a step-by-step table setup.

Part 4: Solutions Key (Soluzioni)

Solution to Exercise 1 (Milan Temperatures):
  1. Sorted Data: 14, 14, 15, 15, 17, 19, 21
  2. Mean: (14+14+15+15+17+19+21) ÷ 7 = 115 ÷ 7 ≈ 16.43 °C
  3. Median: The middle number in the sorted list is 15.
  4. Mode: Both 14 and 15 appear twice! This data is bimodal. Modes are 14 and 15.
  5. Range (Intervallo): 21 − 14 = 7 °C
Solution to Exercise 2 (Pizza Delivery):

1. Mean: (20 + 25 + 22 + 30 + 28) ÷ 5 = 125 ÷ 5 = 25 minutes.

2. Calculation details: Sum of squared distances is (-5)^2 + (-3)^2 + 0^2 + 3^2 + 5^2 = 25 + 9 + 0 + 9 + 25 = 68.
Variance = 68 ÷ 5 = 13.6.
Standard Deviation: √13.6 ≈ 3.69 minutes.

Solution to Exercise 3 (Graph Interpretation):
  1. Figure 3 (Low Variation) has significantly more students scoring exactly 7 (the peak is much taller, representing a higher count/frequency at the center).
  2. Figure 3 has a higher percentage of passing students. In Figure 3, every single student scored between 6 and 8, meaning 100% passed. In Figure 4, multiple students scored 4 and 5, meaning a significant portion failed.
  3. A flat, wide profile means data points are spread out far from the center mean value. Since standard deviation measures the average distance from the mean, a wider distribution inherently yields a larger standard deviation value.
